About me

I am a fourth-year Ph.D. student and Graduate Research Assistant in the School of Computer Science at the Georgia Institute of Technology, advised by Dr. Suguman Bansal. My research lies at the intersection of Formal Methods and Reinforcement Learning, with a focus on building learning-enabled systems that can generalize reliably, be evaluated rigorously, and satisfy formally specified behavior.

My work aims to make reinforcement learning systems more scalable, robust, and trustworthy. I study how formal specifications, logical structure, and verification-inspired techniques can guide the training, evaluation, and certification of learned policies. A central theme of my research is specification-guided generalization i.e. enabling agents to transfer learned behavior across related tasks while preserving correctness and safety-relevant properties.

I am also interested in how large language models can support planning, decision-making, and generalization in interactive environments, especially when combined with formal reasoning and verification tools. My long-term goal is to develop AI systems that are not only capable of learning from interaction, but also able to justify, certify, and reliably adapt their behavior.

Before joining Georgia Tech, I completed my undergraduate studies at the National Institute of Technology Tiruchirappalli and interned at Newcastle University in collaboration with the Max Planck Institute, where I worked with Dr. Sadegh Soudjani on reinforcement learning methods for stochastic max-plus linear systems.

Research Interests

My research focuses on reliable learning-enabled systems: agents that learn, generalize across tasks, and satisfy formally specified behavior.

  1. Formal methods for reinforcement learning and specification-guided generalization.
  2. Verification, certification, and rigorous evaluation of learned policies.
  3. Logical specifications, task structure, and generalization in interactive learning.
  4. LLM-assisted planning, decision-making, and trustworthy autonomous systems.
